Biography

Jose Garcia Villa, born out of Simeon Villa a personal physician of Emilio Aguinaldo and guia Garcia in Singalong district of Manila. Villa enrolled on a Pre-Medical course in the University of the Philippines, but then switched to Pre-Law course. However, he realized that his true passion was in the arts.

At the time, Villa's art poetry style was deemed too harsh. In 1929, he published Man Songs, a collection of sensual poems, in The Philippines Herald magazine supplement, which was deemed too daring by authorities at the University of the Philippines, and he was even penalized for obscenity by the Manila Court of First Instance. That same year, Villa received The Philippines Free Press magazine's Best Story of the Year award for Mir-I-Nisa. He was also awarded P1,000 in prize money, which he utilized to travel to the United States.

He enrolled at the University of New Mexico and was a founding member of Clay, a mimeograph literary magazine. He earned a Bachelor of Arts degree and continued his education at Columbia University. Villa, one of the few Asians to do so at the time, had steadily gained the attention of the country's literary circles. Villa moved from writing prose to poetry after the publication of Footnote to Youth in 1933, and released only a few works until 1942. During the release of Have Come, Am Here in 1942, he established a new rhyming scheme known as "reversed consonance," in which "the last sounded consonants of the last syllable, or the last main consonant of a phrase, are reversed for the corresponding rhyme," according to Villa.

Villa introduced a literary style he coined "comma poems" in 1949, in which commas are added after each word. "The commas are an intrinsic and important part of the medium: regulating the poem's verbal density and time movement: enabling each word to gain a richer tonal value, and the line movement to become more measured," he said in the preface to Volume Two.

From 1949 to 1951, Villa worked as an associate editor for New Directions Publishing in New York City, and from 1952 to 1960, she was the director of the poetry workshop at City College of New York. He then stepped away from the literary scene to focus on teaching, first lecturing at The New School for Social Research from 1964 until 1973 and afterwards giving poetry classes at his apartment. Villa also served as a cultural attaché to the Philippine Mission to the United Nations from 1952 to 1963, and as a cultural adviser to the President of the Philippines beginning in 1968.
